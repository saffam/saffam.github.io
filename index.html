Project 1: Global Health Expenditure Efficiency

Status: Complete | Tools: Python, pandas, matplotlib | Data: WHO Global Health Expenditure Database (2015-2022)

Question: Which countries achieve the most efficient health financing — high government share, low out-of-pocket burden, and sustainable spending relative to GDP?

Key Findings:

  • Spending gap is 280x: US spends $10,701 per capita. Pakistan spends $38.
  • Government share matters: High-income countries (Sweden, Germany, UK) finance 80-90% through government. Low-income countries (Nigeria, India, Pakistan) rely heavily on out-of-pocket payments.
  • US is an outlier: Spends 16.9% of GDP on health — nearly double European levels — with mixed outcomes.
  • European efficiency: Germany, France, Sweden achieve comparable health outcomes at 11-12% of GDP, versus the US at 17%.
  • The out-of-pocket trap: In Nigeria, India, and Pakistan, households bear 70-75% of costs directly, creating access barriers and financial catastrophe.

Policy Implications:

  • For high-income countries: The US model is expensive. European mixed models offer better value.
  • For middle-income countries: Increasing government health share reduces household burden and improves access.
  • For low-income countries: External aid and domestic resource mobilization are critical to escape the out-of-pocket trap.

Project 2: The COVID-19 Health Financing Shock

Status: Complete | Tools: Python, pandas, matplotlib | Data: WHO Global Health Expenditure Database (2019-2021)

Question: When COVID-19 hit, did governments absorb the cost or did households pay more? Which health systems proved resilient vs. fragile?

Key Findings:

  • Resilient systems (Germany, Canada, Japan, France): Increased public health spending by 1.1-1.4% of GDP while reducing household out-of-pocket burden. Government financing expanded to protect citizens.
  • Fragile systems (Nigeria, Russian Federation): Increased spending but households absorbed the cost. Nigeria's out-of-pocket share rose 4.7 percentage points despite spending increases.
  • Stagnant systems (China, Brazil, Pakistan, Australia, Sweden): Minimal spending response to the pandemic. Health systems did not scale financing to meet the crisis.
  • Mixed systems (USA, Italy, Indonesia, Mexico, India, South Africa): Moderate spending increases with varying household impacts. The US increased spending 0.8% of GDP but already had low out-of-pocket share.

Policy Implications:

  • Resilience requires pre-existing public financing capacity: Countries with strong tax-funded systems (Germany, Canada) could mobilize resources quickly. Fragmented systems could not.
  • The out-of-pocket trap worsens during crises: In Nigeria and Russia, emergency spending translated to household costs because public financing infrastructure was weak.
  • Stagnation is a policy choice: China's minimal response suggests either efficient containment or underinvestment in health security. Brazil's stagnation reflects political instability.
  • For digital health: Countries with resilient financing are better positioned to invest in health IT infrastructure. Fragile systems need digital solutions that reduce costs, not add them.

Project 3: Pharmacovigilance Signal Detection — FDA FAERS Q1 2026

Status: Complete | Tools: Python, pandas, matplotlib | Data: FDA Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) Q1 2026

Question: Which drugs show the highest adverse event reporting volume, and what safety signals emerge from real-world post-market surveillance data?

Key Findings:

  • 397,224 adverse event reports analyzed from Q1 2026 — one of the largest publicly available pharmacovigilance datasets.
  • Top reported drugs: Dupixent (42,951), Zepbound (26,791), Mounjaro (22,069). These are all biologics and GLP-1 agonists — the fastest-growing drug categories with limited long-term safety data.
  • Most common reactions: Off-label use (27,774), drug ineffective (22,442), dose omission (19,786). "Off-label use" as the top reaction suggests significant reporting of unapproved indications — a critical signal for regulators.
  • Serious outcomes: 30,927 deaths, 13,222 life-threatening events, 80,509 hospitalizations. The death count alone represents a public health signal requiring systematic review.
  • Biosimilar signal: Inflectra (infliximab biosimilar) shows unusual reporting patterns — high "off-label use" and "intentional product use issue" counts. This suggests potential confusion between originator and biosimilar, or inappropriate switching.

Policy Implications:

  • GLP-1 drugs need enhanced surveillance: Zepbound and Mounjaro are new, high-volume, and reported heavily. The FDA should prioritize post-market studies for cardiovascular outcomes and long-term metabolic effects.
  • Off-label reporting is a signal, not noise: When "off-label use" is the top reaction, it indicates either widespread unapproved use or reporting bias. Both require regulatory attention.
  • Biosimilar interchangeability needs monitoring: Inflectra's pattern suggests biosimilar switching may not be as seamless as assumed. Pharmacovigilance systems must track biosimilar-specific signals.
  • For digital health: Real-world evidence platforms can augment FAERS by capturing outcomes from EHRs and patient apps, reducing reliance on voluntary reporting.
